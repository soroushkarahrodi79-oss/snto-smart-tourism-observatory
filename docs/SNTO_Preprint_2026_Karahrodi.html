SNTO: A Satellite-Based Territorial Intelligence Framework for Proactive, Regenerative Governance of Protected-Area Tourism

Abstract

Protected natural areas in Spain and across Europe largely manage tourism impact reactively, intervening only once trail degradation is visually evident on the ground. This paper presents the Smart Nature Tourism Observatory (SNTO), an open-source territorial intelligence framework that converts freely available Sentinel-2 remote sensing data (NDVI + NDMI, Copernicus programme) into proactive, actionable management intelligence for national parks and protected areas.

The framework introduces three core contributions. First, a scene-percentile-calibrated Ecological Health Score (EHS) that anchors each trail's health to the empirical pixel distribution of the actual Sentinel-2 scene rather than to fixed spectral thresholds, making the index robust to inter-seasonal and inter-territory variability. Second, a Spatial Causal Model (SCM) that partitions observed degradation into use-driven (trail-proximal, 0–50 m buffer) and climate-driven (landscape-scale, 200–1000 m) components through a spatial impact gradient, directly informing whether restoration budget should be locally assigned. Third, a Decision Confidence Score (DCS) that prevents issuing spending recommendations when data quality or temporal robustness fall below minimum thresholds.

Applied to the Parque Nacional Sierra de Guadarrama (PNSG), Spain — using official OAPN cartography and two real Sentinel-2 scenes (summer 2025-08-10 and spring 2026-04-10, tile T30TVL) — the system analysed 73 trails (149.4 km) and identified 17 (23%) with active seasonal deterioration (ΔEHS > 0). Causal attribution classified 27 trails as localised-impact (use-driven), 9 as mixed, and 37 as landscape-driven (climate-forcing dominant). The indicative restoration budget under the causal-weighted model amounts to approximately €205,000.

The system is explicitly bounded: with two scenes it provides seasonal early-warning signals, not multi-year trends. All code (493 automated tests), data provenance labels, and limitations are openly documented. The framework aligns with European protected-area reporting standards (Natura 2000 / EUROPARC / CETS).

Keywords: remote sensing; Sentinel-2; NDVI; NDMI; protected areas; sustainable tourism; causal attribution; territorial intelligence; Sierra de Guadarrama; Spain; OAPN; open science

1. Introduction

Tourism is both a primary economic driver and a leading source of ecological stress for protected natural areas worldwide. In Spain, the national park network (Red de Parques Nacionales, OAPN) receives over 15 million visits per year, with the Sierra de Guadarrama National Park (PNSG) — the closest national park to Madrid — among the most visited mountain ecosystems in Southern Europe. Trail degradation, vegetation loss and soil compaction are well-documented consequences of sustained visitor pressure, yet management agencies consistently report acting reactively: interventions occur only after damage becomes visible on the ground, when restoration is more costly and sometimes impossible [1,2].

Earth Observation (EO) data from the Copernicus Sentinel-2 programme offers a cost-effective pathway to proactive monitoring: 10-m multispectral imagery with a 5-day revisit cycle is freely available globally. Spectral vegetation indices — chiefly NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) and NDMI (Normalized Difference Moisture Index) — have been validated as proxies for trail-level vegetation health and soil moisture at mountain and Mediterranean sites [3,4,5]. However, translating raw spectral signals into decision-relevant indicators for park managers requires addressing three outstanding gaps:

  1. Cross-scene comparability. Fixed spectral thresholds are not transferable across seasons, territories or phenological regimes without recalibration.
  2. Causal attribution. Vegetation stress observed at trail scale may originate from visitor pressure or from regional climate forcing (drought, heat stress); the management response — and whether local restoration budgets are warranted — differs fundamentally.
  3. Decision confidence. Satellite-derived recommendations must communicate uncertainty honestly and withhold actionable advice when data quality is insufficient.

This paper presents the Smart Nature Tourism Observatory (SNTO), an open-source framework that addresses all three gaps through a two-pipeline architecture applied to the PNSG as the primary test territory.

2. Study Area

The Parque Nacional Sierra de Guadarrama (PNSG) covers approximately 33,960 ha along the central Iberian mountain range between the provinces of Madrid and Segovia (Spain). Designated as a national park in 2013, it spans altitudes from ~900 m to 2,428 m (Peñalara peak) and supports diverse ecosystems including Scots pine forests, montane scrublands, subalpine grasslands and glacial lakes. The park is governed by the Organismo Autónomo de Parques Nacionales (OAPN, Ministry for Ecological Transition) under a Plan Rector de Uso y Gestión (PRUG) that zones activities into core, restricted and service areas.

We analysed 73 named trails reconstructed from the official OAPN cartography supplemented with OpenStreetMap (OSM Overpass, named ways dissolved by name), representing 149.4 km of formal visitor infrastructure. The study covers 34 municipalities in the park's zone of influence, for which socioeconomic data were obtained from the INE national census and the ALMUDENA municipal data bank of the Comunidad de Madrid.

As a calibration pilot, the Reserva de la Biosfera Sierra del Rincón (Madrid province, ~16,000 ha, UNESCO Biosphere Reserve 1992) was also processed with independent Sentinel-2 scenes to validate the EHS methodology under different ecological and phenological conditions.

Sentinel-2 scenes were downloaded as .SAFE product archives. Scene Classification Layer (SCL) masks were applied to exclude cloud, shadow and non-vegetated pixels from percentile calculations.

4. Methodology

4.1 Ecological Health Score (EHS)

For each trail, a 50-m buffer was generated and raster statistics (mean NDVI, mean NDMI) were extracted using rasterstats. Rather than applying global spectral thresholds, we anchor each score to the empirical pixel distribution of the specific scene, using the 90th percentile as the healthy reference (P90) and the 10th percentile as the degraded floor (P10). This makes EHS comparable across seasons without requiring external calibration data.

Dx = clamp((P90 − x̄) / (P90 − P10), 0, 1)

EHS = 100 · (1 − (wNDVI · DNDVI + wNDMI · DNDMI))

where wNDVI = wNDMI = 0.5 (standard mode); in dense-canopy (NDVI > 0.80): wNDVI = 0.20, wNDMI = 0.80

EHS ranges from 0 (worst) to 100 (healthiest). Seasonal change is quantified as ΔEHS = EHSsummer − EHSspring; a positive ΔEHS (summer healthier than spring) indicates improvement; a negative ΔEHS indicates seasonal deterioration. The dense-canopy NDVI saturation guard shifts weight toward NDMI for trails running through beech forests (Hayedos), where closed-canopy conditions render NDVI insensitive to sub-canopy stress.

4.2 Spatial Causal Model (SCM)

To partition degradation by cause, we compute a Spatial Impact Gradient (SIG) that contrasts spectral health at three radial zones around each trail centreline: core (0–50 m, direct trail influence), near (50–200 m, transition zone), and landscape (200–1000 m, background climatic signal).

SIG = (NDVIlandscape − NDVIcore) / max(NDVIlandscape, 0.01)

SIG ≥ 0.15 → LOCALIZED_IMPACT (use-driven; causal factor = 1.0)
0.07 ≤ SIG < 0.15 → MIXED (causal factor = 0.5)
SIG < 0.07 → LANDSCAPE_DRIVEN (climate; causal factor = 0.0)

The causal factor modulates the restoration budget following a polluter-pays principle: only the fraction of degradation attributable to visitor pressure justifies local investment. Climate-driven degradation requires a different policy response (climate adaptation, habitat management) and is excluded from the local budget.

4.3 Decision Confidence Score (DCS)

DCS aggregates five dimensions on a 0–100 scale: Data Quality (DQ, max 25), Temporal Robustness (TR, max 25), Spatial Consistency (SC, max 20), Model Stability (MS, max 15), Signal Strength (SS, max 15). Two hard gates prevent issuing actionable recommendations: DQ < 10 or TR < 12 sets can_act = False regardless of the total score. With only two scenes, TR is limited by design and DCS is transparently reported as an early-warning system.

4.4 Budget Prioritisation (TPI / TIS)

The Territorial Priority Index (TPI) combines ecological risk (0.40), human pressure (0.30) and socioeconomic vulnerability (0.30) to rank trails into four investment tiers (I Immediate → IV Promotion). The Territorial Impact Score (TIS) integrates environmental, economic and evidence dimensions (weights 0.55 / 0.30 / 0.15) to estimate restoration cost, expected impact and the counterfactual cost of inaction. Unit restoration cost is parameterised at 15.50 €/m (TRAGSA 2023 indicative tariff; subject to official verification), modulated by the SCM causal factor.

4.5 Socioeconomic Vulnerability Layer

For each of the 34 municipalities in the PNSG zone of influence, we compute a Socioeconomic Vulnerability Index (SVI):

SVI = 0.40 · DEP + 0.30 · DEM + 0.30 · ENV

where DEP = tourism dependency (EOATR occupancy proxy), DEM = demographic fragility (ageing index + depopulation trend), ENV = environmental exposure (trail-level EHS risk aggregated to municipality). Jobs at risk are estimated as hospitality employment (ALMUDENA affiliation data) × ENV exposure weight. All socioeconomic outputs are flagged as prospective scenarios, not measured quantities.

5.1 Ecological Health Score

Mean EHS across the 73 PNSG trails was 27.6/100 in the summer scene (maximum 71.3; minimum 3.2). Seventeen trails (23%) showed active seasonal deterioration (ΔEHS > 0, mean ΔEHS among deteriorating trails = −17.8), indicating that summer vegetation health was lower than spring, consistent with heat/drought stress compounded by visitor pressure during the peak season (July–August). The remaining 56 trails showed stable or improving EHS across the two scenes.

Six trails in the Tier I (Immediate Intervention) category — including Laguna de Peñalara, Cumbre de los Siete Picos and Cuerda Larga — combined EHS values below 40 with LOCALIZED_IMPACT classification, representing the highest-confidence candidates for restoration intervention. Three of these triggered the Intervención Crítica alert threshold (EHS < 40 and ΔEHS > 15).

5.2 Causal Attribution

SCM classification of the 73 trails: 27 LOCALIZED_IMPACT (37%), 9 MIXED (12%), 37 LANDSCAPE_DRIVEN (51%). The majority of trails (51%) showed a landscape-scale spectral gradient consistent with climate forcing rather than trail-proximal use pressure. This result is consistent with the documented 2025 drought anomaly in the Iberian Peninsula [6] and underscores the importance of causal attribution before allocating local restoration budgets.

5.3 Socioeconomic Layer

Across 34 municipalities, mean SVI was 0.52 (range 0.28–0.81). The highest vulnerability municipalities — Rascafría, Lozoya and Navacerrada — combine high tourism dependency (EOATR data), accelerating demographic decline and direct proximity to trails in the Tier I / II categories. Estimated hospitality jobs at risk (environmental exposure × affiliation base) total approximately 769, with the caveat that this figure represents a scenario output, not a measured count.

6. Discussion

6.1 Strengths

The primary methodological contribution of SNTO is the decoupling of health quantification (EHS) from cause attribution (SCM). Most existing remote sensing studies of trail degradation focus on spectral change detection without addressing whether that change is driven by use or by climate variability [7,8]. By making causal attribution an explicit, auditable step that controls budget allocation, SNTO prevents misallocation of restoration funds to landscape-scale climate signals that cannot be addressed locally.

The scene-percentile calibration of EHS (using P90/P10 of the actual scene) avoids the need for external reference datasets, making the framework applicable to territories without long vegetation-monitoring records — a common constraint in Spanish national parks.

6.2 Limitations and Honest Bounds

Temporal scope: With two Sentinel-2 scenes (one summer, one spring), SNTO provides a seasonal early-warning signal (ΔEHS), not a multi-year trend. Mann-Kendall trend detection — implemented in Pipeline B — requires a minimum of 4 observations and is statistically robust only beyond 10 data points [9]. Results from this study should not be cited as evidence of long-term degradation trends.
Validation: No field validation campaign (penetrometer readings, ground cover surveys, erosion measurements) has yet been conducted. The SCM causal thresholds (SIG ≥ 0.15 for LOCALIZED, ≤ 0.07 for LANDSCAPE) are calibrated from the spectral data alone and require corroboration against in-situ measurements of visitor-impact indicators [10].
Budget figures: The restoration cost estimate (€205,000) is an indicative order-of-magnitude figure based on a unit rate of 15.50 €/m (TRAGSA 2023). It should not be used for procurement or official budget planning without verification against current official tariffs and site-specific engineering assessments.
Socioeconomic outputs: All employment and income figures are scenario projections derived from proxy relationships (hospitality affiliation × environmental exposure). They are not measured economic impacts.

6.3 Alignment with Policy Frameworks

SNTO aligns with European reporting requirements for protected areas, specifically Natura 2000 conservation status monitoring, the EUROPARC European Charter for Sustainable Tourism (CETS) — particularly Principles 3 (natural heritage protection) and 10 (visitor flow management) — and the SISMOTUR sustainable tourism indicators framework. The framework's open code and provenance documentation are designed to facilitate institutional adoption and independent audit.

7. Conclusion

We have presented SNTO, an open-source territorial intelligence framework that converts freely available Sentinel-2 imagery into actionable health assessments, causal attribution and budget prioritisation for protected-area trail management. Applied to the Sierra de Guadarrama National Park with official OAPN cartography, the system identified 17 of 73 trails (23%) with active seasonal deterioration and allocated a causal-weighted indicative restoration budget of ~€205,000, of which only 37% of trails were classified as use-driven — a finding that would have been invisible without explicit causal attribution.

The framework is reproducible (493 automated tests, open repository), scalable to other territories in the Spanish national park network (OAPN), and designed for honest communication of uncertainty to multiple stakeholder audiences (technical, management, institutional). The next development stage requires a multi-year Sentinel-2 time series (via Google Earth Engine) to activate trend detection, and a ground-truth campaign to validate the SCM causal thresholds and close the pseudo-validation loop.
